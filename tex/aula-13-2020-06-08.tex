\section{The ideal gas law and specific heats for a pure substance}\label{sec:pVnRT-pure}

The ideal gas law applies well for typical conditions of 
the troposphere. For a \emph{pure substance}, it is\index{
ideal gas law} 
\begin{equation}
  pV = nR^{\#}T,\label{eq:tudo}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is pressure, $V$ is the volume occupied by the 
gas, $n$ is the number of moles, $R^{\#} = 8.314\,462\,
618 \,15\, \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ (in the SI) is t
he u niversal gas constant\index{gas constant!universal }
, and $T$ is thermodynamic temperature. Still for a pure 
substance, (\ref{eq:tudo}) can also be written as 
\begin{align}
a &= b, \nonumber \\
p &= n\frac{R^{\#}}{V} T 
   = \frac{m}{M}\frac{R^{\#}}{V} T
   = \frac{m}{V}\frac{R^{\#}}{M} T\;\Rightarrow\nonumber\\
p &= \rho R T. \label{eq:prhoRt}
\end{align}
Above, $M$ is the molar mass\index{molar mass}; $m$ is the mass of the gas, which is related to the number of
moles by
\begin{equation}
m = nM; \label{eq:mnM}
\end{equation}
the gas constant is
\begin{equation}
R = \frac{R^{\#}}{M},\label{eq:RRM}
\end{equation}
which depends on the specific gas through $M$ in
\ref{eq:RRM}; and
\begin{equation}
\rho = \frac{m}{V} \label{eq:def-rho}
\end{equation}
is the density; its reciprocal 
\begin{equation}
  \vsp = \frac{1}{\rho}\label{eq:def-vsp}
\end{equation}
is the \emph{specific volume} $\vsp$. Note that (\ref{eq:prhoRt}) is
more useful than (\ref{eq:tudo}) in practice: $V$ does not
appear in it, and it only involves intensive quantities (pressure,
density and temperature), so it applies at every point in the atmosphere.






The perfect gas law in the form (\ref{eq:tudo}) or (\ref{eq:prhoRt})
is an example of a state equation in
thermodynamics. For a \emph{pure substance},
then, the state equation takes one of the forms
\[
p = p(\vsp,T) \qquad\text{or}\qquad 
\vsp = \vsp(p,T) \qquad \text{or}\qquad
T = T(p,\vsp).
\]
For atmospheric gases, the lower case letters indicate specific
quantities (per unit mass or unit volume), and are more convenient since we will not usually
treat individual ``systems'' or ``lumps'' of air.

Two important thermodynamical functions are the specific internal energy
$\ine$ and the specific enthalpy $\ent$ defined by
\begin{equation}
\ent = \ine + p\vsp. \label{eq:def-ent}
\end{equation}
For a pure substance,
they are functions of two variables, chosen between $p$, 
$\vsp$ and $T$. We usually write
\begin{align}
\ine &= \ine(\vsp,T), \label{eq:ein}\\
\ent &= \ent(p,T), \label{eq:ent}
\end{align}
since these pairs lead to useful definitions of measurable quantities such as
specific heats.  Equations (\ref{eq:ein}) and (\ref{eq:ent}) lead to perfect
differentials

The specific heats at constant volume and constant temperature are defined by
\begin{align}
c_{\vsp} &= \left(\parder{\ine}{T}\right)_{\vsp}, \label{eq:def-cv}\\
c_p &= \left(\parder{\ent}{T}\right)_p. \label{eq:def-cp}
\end{align}
The subscripts $\vsp$ and $p$ on the right-hand side of the equations above are used
in thermodynamics as reminders that $\ine$ is being taken as a function of
$\vsp,T$, and that $\ent$ is being taken as a function of $p,T$.

For a perfect gas, from (\ref{eq:prhoRt}) and (\ref{eq:def-vsp}), the
equation of state takes the form
\begin{equation}
p\vsp = RT, \label{eq:pvRT}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is a gas constant specific for the pure substance and it is
possible to show that in this case $\ine$ and $\ent$ are functions of
$T$ only \citep{adkins--equilibrium}. Then,
(\ref{eq:def-cv})--(\ref{eq:def-cp}) become ordinary
derivatives. Also,
\begin{align}
\left(\parder{\ent}{T}\right)_p &=
    \left(\parder{}{T}\left[ \ine + p\vsp\right]\right)_p\nonumber \\
    &= \left(\parder{\ine}{T}\right)_p + p\left(\parder{\vsp}{T}\right)_p\nonumber \\
    &= \mderiva{\ine}{T}+ p\frac{R}{p}\;\Rightarrow\nonumber\\
c_p &= c_{\vsp} + R. \label{eq:cpcvR}
\end{align}
Physically, $c_p > c_{\vsp}$ due to the extra energy necessary for the expansion of
  the air against the constant pressure $p$.





\section{The atmosphere as a mixture of ideal gases}\label{sec:mix-gases}

The atmosphere however is not a pure substance, but a mixture of
gases. The question then is how (\ref{eq:tudo}) can be reasonably
extended to account for mixtures.  In fact, from now on, often when we
use one of (\ref{eq:tudo})--(\ref{eq:def-vsp}), we will be referring
to the total atmospheric pressure $p$ (of a mixture of gases), the
total density $\rho$, etc..



Therefore, we start by requiring that (\ref{eq:tudo}) \emph{apply for
  the mixture}; then, the total number of moles, $n$, is the sum of
the number of moles of each individual constituent. Let $i$ be the
index for each gas in a mixture; we have
\begin{equation}
n = \sum_{i,j,k} n_i,\label{eq:total-n}
\end{equation}
where $n_i$ is the number of moles of gas $i$.

We want the perfect gas law to apply for each constituent as well, and
if we substitute (\ref{eq:total-n}) into (\ref{eq:tudo}), the question
is what to do with the left-hand side of (\ref{eq:tudo}). There are
two possibilities: the model of partial pressures, and the model of
partial volumes. In the first case, we specify for each gas
\begin{equation}
p_i V = n_i R^{\#}T.\label{eq:gas-pi}\\
\end{equation}
In this model all gases occupy a common volume $V$, each one exerting its
own partial pressure $p_i$, such that the total 
pressure is
\begin{equation}
p = \sum_{i} p_{i}.\label{eq:sum-p-chi}
\end{equation}
In the second case,
\begin{equation}
pV_i = n_i R^{\#} T,\label{eq:gas-vi}
\end{equation}
and now all gases are subject to the same pressure $p$, each one
occuping, nominally, a partial volume $V_i$, with
\begin{equation}
V = \sum_{i} V_i.\label{eq:sum-V-chi}
\end{equation}


In both cases, summing the individual equations of state recovers the
perfect gas law for the mixture. For example, starting from
(\ref{eq:gas-pi}),
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i} \left( p_i V \right) &= \sum_i \left(n_i R^{\#}T\right),\\
  \left(\sum_{i} p_i  \right)V &= \left( \sum_i  n_i\right)R^{\#}T,\\
  p V &= nR^{\#}T
\end{align*}
(recovering (\ref{eq:tudo})), and similarly for (\ref{eq:gas-vi}).



From (\ref{eq:tudo}), (\ref{eq:gas-pi}) and (\ref{eq:gas-vi}), it
follows that
\begin{equation}
x_i = \frac{p_i}{p} 
    = \frac{V_i}{V} 
	= \frac{n_i}{n}\label{eq:fracoes-gas}
\end{equation}
defines a concentration (traditionally expressed in \%,
parts per million (ppm), parts per billion (ppb), etc.) either in
partial pressure fraction, partial volume fraction, or \emph{mole
  fraction}.  
The gas law for each constituent can also be written
\begin{align}
p_i &= n_i\frac{R^{\#}}{V} T 
       = \frac{m_i}{M_i}\frac{R^{\#}}{V} T
       = \frac{m_i}{V}\frac{R^{\#}}{M_i} T\;\Rightarrow\nonumber\\
p_i &= \rho_i R_i T,\label{eq:prhoRt-chi}
\end{align}



where (as before for a pure substance)
\begin{equation}
m_i = n_i M_i \label{eq:mnMi}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{align}
  \rho_i &= m_i/V,\label{eq:def-rho-chi}\\
  \vsp_i &= \frac{1}{\rho_i},\label{eq:def-vsp-chi}
\end{align}
are the density or the specific mass (with mass $m_i$ occuping the
volume $V$) and the specific volume respectively of the gas $i$,
and $M_i$ is the molar mass of gas $i$. In (\ref{eq:prhoRt-chi}),
\begin{equation}
R_i = \frac{R^{\#}}{M_i} \label{eq:Rchidef}
\end{equation}
is the gas constant for gas $i$.

Once more,  (\ref{eq:prhoRt}) holds for the
mixture. To see how, note that the total mass of the mixture is
\begin{equation}
  m = \sum_{i} m_i;\label{eq:mass-total}
\end{equation}
and similarly for the total density:
\begin{equation}
\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{\sum_i m_i}{V} = \sum_i \frac{m_i}{V} =
\sum_i \rho_i.\label{eq:rho-total}
\end{equation}
We will also need to define the \emph{mass concentration}, or
\emph{mass fraction} of each
constituent,
\begin{equation}
c_i \equiv \frac{m_i}{m}=\frac{\rho_i}{\rho}.\label{eq:def-c-chi}
\end{equation}\index{concentration!mass}\index{fraction!mass}
With (\ref{eq:mass-total})--(\ref{eq:rho-total}) in hand, we sum
(\ref{eq:prhoRt-chi}) to obtain
\begin{align*}
  \sum_{i} p_i &= \sum_{i} \rho_i R_i T,\\
             p &= \left( \sum_{i} \rho_i R_i \right)T \equiv \rho R T.
\end{align*}
This \emph{defines} the gas constant $R$ of the mixture; now
\begin{align}
  R &= \frac{\sum_{i} \rho_i R_i}{\rho}\nonumber \\
    &= \frac{\sum_{i} \frac{m_i}{V}R_i}{\frac{m}{V}}\nonumber\\
    &= \sum_{i} \frac{m_i}{m} R_i = \sum_{i} c_i R_i. \label{eq:cchi-Rrchi}
\end{align}\index{gas constant!of a mixture}
Thus, given the mass concentrations, we can calculate the equivalent
gas constant.

The \emph{mean molar mass} of the mixture is
\begin{equation}
  M = \sum_{i} x_i M_i = \frac{\sum_i n_i M_i}{n} = \frac{m}{n},\label{eq:mnMagain}
\end{equation}\index{molar mass!mean}
which recovers (\ref{eq:mnM}). This finally allows us to close the
circuit to recover (\ref{eq:RRM}) for the mixture. From (\ref{eq:cchi-Rrchi}),
\begin{align}
  R &= \frac{1}{m} \left( \sum_i m_i R_i\right)  \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{1}{m} \left( \sum_i n_i M_i R_i\right)  \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{1}{m} \left( \sum_i n_i R^{\#}\right) = \frac{1}{m}
       \left( \sum_i n_i\right) R^{\#} \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{n}{m} R^{\#}  = \frac{R^{\#}}{M}, \label{eq:RRMagain}
\end{align}
by virtue of (\ref{eq:mnMagain}), which recovers (\ref{eq:RRM}).



\section{Concentrations}\label{sec:concentrations}

\index{concentration}



A relationship between $x_i$ and $c_i$ can now be readily obtained:
\begin{equation*}
  x_i = \frac{n_i}{n} = \frac{\frac{m_i}{M_i}}{\frac{m}{M}} = \frac{m_i}{m}\frac{M}{M_i},
\end{equation*}
or
\begin{equation}
x_i M_i = c_i M.\label{eq:xici}
\end{equation}




Other concentration measures are also in use in meteorology, and need
to be defined. The \emph{mixing ratio}\index{mixing ratio} can be expressed either as
a \emph{mole ratio},
\begin{equation}
  \eta_i = \frac{n_i}{n - n_i},\label{eq:def-mole-ratio}
\end{equation}\index{mixing ratio!mole ratio}
or as a \emph{mass ratio}
\begin{equation}
  r_i = \frac{m_i}{m - m_i}.\label{eq:def-mass-ratio}
\end{equation}\index{mixing ratio!mass ratio}
It is easy to convert mass fractions
$c_i$ to mixing (mass) ratios $r_i$ and vice-versa:
\begin{align}
  r_i &= \frac{\rho_i}{\rho - \rho_i} 
      = \frac{\frac{\rho_i}{\rho}}{1 - \frac{\rho_i}{\rho}};\nonumber\\
  r_i &= \frac{c_i}{1 - c_i}\; \Rightarrow \label{eq:citori}\\
  c_i &= \frac{r_i}{1 + r_i}.\label{eq:ritoci}
\end{align}

Um exemplo de \texttt{align}, e um exemplo de \texttt{align*}:
\begin{align}
   A &= B, \label{eq:a.eq.b}\\
   C &= D, \nonumber\\
   E &= F  \label{eq:mosquinhaazul}
\end{align}
\begin{align*}
   A &= B, \\
   C &= D, \\
   E &= F  
\end{align*}


The mixing ratio, therefore, is the ratio of the amount (either in
moles or in mass) of substance $i$ to the amount of all \emph{other}
substances. It follows that for any substance \emph{other than} water
vapor, the denominator includes the amount of water vapor present in
the air at the time of measurement. For this reason, \emph{when $i$ is not
water vapor}, (\ref{eq:def-mole-ratio})--(\ref{eq:def-mass-ratio}) are
often called \emph{wet mixing ratios}.

\section{Moist air}\label{sec:moist-air}


Note that the equations of section \ref{sec:pVnRT-pure} are not
strictly valid for atmospheric air, which is not a \emph{pure}
substance; rather, it is a mixture of many gases. Each new constituent
makes the thermodynamical equations more complex; in particular,
functions of state such as $\ine$ and $\ent$ then become also
functions of the concentrations of each new constituent.



We will not follow the rigorous path of treating the atmosphere as a
mixture of several gases and writing down the full thermodynamical
equations for that mixture; instead, we will consider ``dry air'',
discussed in sections \ref{sec:mix-gases}--\ref{sec:concentrations}
and will introduce various humidity indices for the concentration of
water vapor (the most important varying component). 

Consider now a certain volume $V$, composed of initally dry air with
mass $m_d$, total number of moles $n_d$, pressure $p_{d}$, and density
$\rho_d$, to which is added water vapor with mass $m_v$ and $n_v$
moles.  The equations developed in section \ref{sec:mix-gases} apply
for moist air. Following (\ref{eq:gas-pi}), each gas component exerts
its own partial pressure as a funcion of temperature $T$ and number of
moles $n_i$. If water vapor is added isothermically, the total
pressure after its addition will increase by \emph{the partial
  pressure of water vapor}, which, from (\ref{eq:gas-pi}), is
\begin{equation}
  e = \frac{1}V n_v R^{\#} T.
\end{equation}
Strictly speaking, we should have used $p_v$ instead of $e$ above, but
it is standard in meteorology to call the partial pressure of water
vapor $e$, and we will follow that practice.

The water vapor content of the air, however, is highly variable in 
the atmosphere; also, in scalar turbulent flux measurements it is 
often better to employ concentrations measured in (an equivalent) dry 
atmosphere \cite[see, for   example,][]{butenhoff2002correction}. 
Therefore, we revisit several concentration defintions with respect 
to the \emph{dry} components only.  For the mole fraction we now have 
the \emph{dry} pressure, volume and molar ratios 
\begin{equation}
  x_{di} = \frac{p_i}{p_d} = \frac{V_i}{V_d} =
  \frac{n_i}{n_d}\qquad (i \ne v)\label{eq:xdi}
\end{equation}
and the \emph{dry} mass fraction or mass concentration,
\begin{equation}
c_{di} = \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_d}\qquad (i\ne v).\label{eq:cdi}
\end{equation}
Above, note that after addition of water vapor, the volume of the
\emph{dry} constituents will be $V_d < V$.
We also have the \emph{dry} mixing ratios
\begin{equation}
  \eta_{di} = \frac{n_i}{n_d - n_i},\qquad (i\ne v)\label{eq:def-dry-mole-ratio}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  r_{di} = \frac{m_i}{m_d - m_i}\qquad (i\ne v).\label{eq:def-dry-mass-ratio}
\end{equation}

Provided the relative concentrations of the dry components remain
unaltered, the previous calculations of dry air properities, $M_d$ and
$R_d$ (the mean molecular mass and the gas constant of dry air,
respectively) also hold in a moist atmosphere. Running the risk of
being tedious, let us verify this. The total pressure now is
\begin{equation}
  p = p_d + e.\label{eq:p-in-moist}
\end{equation}
where $p_d$ is calculated as
\begin{equation}
  p_d =  \sum_{i\ne v} p_i = \sum_{i\ne v} \rho_i R_i T \equiv \rho_d R_d T.
  \label{eq:Rd-def-again}
\end{equation}
As before, (\ref{eq:Rd-def-again}) defines $R_d$. Therefore, the value
of the dry gas constant in a moist atmosphere is
\begin{equation}
  R_d = \frac{\sum_{i \ne v} \rho_i R_i}{\rho_d}
      = \sum_{i \ne v} \frac{\rho_i}{\rho_d} R_i 
	  = \sum_{i \ne v} c_{di}R_i,
      \label{eq:Rd-in-moist}
\end{equation}
where the weights of the specific gas constants $R_i$ are now the dry
mass concentrations $c_{di}$.   Proceeding,
\begin{align}
  R_d &= \frac{1}{m_d} \sum_{i \ne v} m_i R_i \nonumber \\
  &= \frac{1}{m_d} \sum_{i \ne v} n_i M_i R_i\nonumber \\
  &= \frac{1}{m_d} \sum_{i \ne v} n_i R^{\#} = \frac{1}{m_d} \left(
  \sum_{i\ne v} n_i\right)R^{\#}\nonumber \\
  &= \frac{n_d}{m_d}R^{\#}\label{eq:Rd-from-ndmd-moist}
\end{align}
But the mean molar mass of dry air in the moist atmosphere is, by
definition,
\begin{equation}
  M_d = \sum_{i \ne v} x_{di} M_i = \frac{\sum_{i\ne v}n_i M_i}{n_d}
  =  
  \frac{m_d}{n_d}
  \label{eq:Md-in-moist}
\end{equation}
so that, as before,
\begin{equation}
  R_d = \frac{R^{\#}}{M_d}.\label{eq:RRMd}
\end{equation}
Also note that if the relative concentrations of the dry components do
not change, then the $x_{di}$'s in (\ref{eq:Md-in-moist}) are the
same as the $x_i$'s in table \ref{tab:ppgas}, which proves the assertion that
in this case the values of $M_d$ and $R_d$ do not change.




With these results in hand, we can simplify calculations substantially
by dividing air occupying $V$ with total mass $m$ into a dry component
and water vapor, \textit{viz.}
\begin{align}
  m &= m_d + m_v, \nonumber \\
\frac{m}{V} &= \frac{m_d}{V} + \frac{m_v}{V}, \nonumber \\
\rho &= \rho_d + \rho_v,\label{eq:total-approx-rho}
\end{align}
where $\rho_v$ is the water vapor density.  In itself, $\rho_v$ is an
index of water vapor concentration; in meteorology, it is called
\emph{the absolute humidity}. Using (\ref{eq:Rchidef}) with $M_v =
\mathrm{18.0153\, \mathrm{g\,mol^{-1}}}$, we find that
\begin{equation}
R_v=461.5230\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}\label{eq:Rvvalue}
\end{equation}
is the gas constant for water vapor\index{gas constant!water vapor}.
From (\ref{eq:prhoRt-chi}), the partial pressure of water vapor is
\begin{equation}
e = \rho_v R_v T. \label{eq:par-e}
\end{equation}




The \emph{specific humidity} $q$ is the same as the mass fraction for
water vapor, $c_v$ (see (\ref{eq:def-c-chi})) and is defined as
\begin{equation}
q = \frac{\rho_v}{\rho}. \label{eq:def-sp-hum}
\end{equation}
Again, in meteorology it is standard to use $q$ and not $c_v$ (the latter
is also easy to confuse with the specific heat at constant volue,
$c_\vsp$, and will be avoided).
Summing (\ref{eq:Rd-def-again}) and (\ref{eq:par-e}), one obtains
\begin{align}
 p &= \left( \rho_d R_d + \rho_v R_v\right)T\nonumber\\
   &= \rho\left( \frac{\rho_d}{\rho}R_d + \frac{\rho_v}{\rho}R_v\right)T\nonumber\\
   &= \rho R_d\left( \frac{\rho_d}{\rho} + \frac{\rho_v}{\rho}\frac{R_v}{R_d}\right)T\nonumber\\
   &= \rho R_d\left( (1-q) + \frac{R_v}{R_d}q\right)T. \label{eq:PRdvdT}
\end{align}


Replacing
\begin{align}
\frac{R_v}{R_d} &= 1.608 \approx 1.61 \;\Rightarrow\nonumber\\
p &= \rho R_d\underbrace{(1 + 0.61q)T}_{T_v}.\label{eq:Tvdef}
\end{align}


The \emph{virtual temperature} $T_v$ defined above is the temperature
of a dry atmosphere with the same density $\rho$; it is slightly
greater than $T$. A further index atmospheric moisture is the mixing
ratio (more specifically, the mass ratio for water vapor). The mixing
ratio was introduced in section \ref{sec:concentrations}; see
(\ref{eq:def-mole-ratio})--(\ref{eq:def-mass-ratio}). For
water vapor, the (mass) mixing ratio is
\begin{equation}
 r_v = \frac{\rho_v}{\rho_d}.\label{eq:mix-ratio}
\end{equation}



Useful relations for the mixing ratio and specific humidity can be
derived as follows:
\begin{align}
r_v &= \frac{\rho_v}{\rho_d} = \frac{\frac{e}{R_vT}}{\frac{(p-e)}{R_dT}}\nonumber\\
  &= \frac{R_d}{R_v}\frac{e}{p-e} = 0.622\,\frac{e}{p-e};\label{eq:rep}\\
q &= \frac{\rho_v}{\rho} = \frac{\frac{e}{R_vT}}{\rho_d + \rho_v}\nonumber\\
  &= \frac{\frac{e}{R_vT}}{\frac{p-e}{R_dT} + \frac{e}{R_vT}}\nonumber\\
  &= \frac{R_d}{R_v} \frac{e}{p + \left(\frac{R_d}{R_v} - 1\right)e}\nonumber\\
  &= 0.622\frac{e}{p - 0.378e}\approx 0.622\frac{e}{p}.\label{eq:qep}
\end{align}




The latent heat of evaporation of water, $L_w$, is the amount of
energy used to change the phase of a unit of mass from liquid to
vapor, in a pure mixture of the two phases. A good approximation for
obtaining an expression for $L_w$ as a function of temperature is the
assumption that the heat capacities of the two phases are equal. This
leads to \cite[Chap. 10, eq. 10.16]{adkins--equilibrium}
\begin{equation}
\md{L_w} =  [c_{pv} - c_{pw}]\md{T}
\end{equation}
where $c_{pv}$ and $c_{pw}$ are the specific heats at constant pressure of
water vapor and liquid water, respectively. If the specific heats, in turn, are
assumed to be constant, we obtain
\begin{equation}
L_w = a_L + b_L T\label{eq:Lw-theo}
\end{equation}
with $b_L = [c_{pv} - c_{pw}]$.  In the SI, with $T$ in Kelvins,
\cite{dake1972evaporative} gives
\begin{equation}
L_w = 3.142689\times 10^{6} - 2.365601\times 10^{3}T\label{eq:Lw-num}
\end{equation}
in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.
\cite{henderson1984new} argues that a more accurate expression is
\begin{equation}
L_w = 1.91846\times 10^6\left[T/(T-33.91)\right]^2. \label{eq:Lw-henderson}
\end{equation}
A reasonable alternative is to use a constant value, given the very
small variation of $L_w$ with $T$. At $T=\mathrm{288.15\,K}$,
(\ref{eq:Lw-num}) gives $L_w = 2.464\times
10^{6}\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.


For a system composed of water vapor and liquid water in equilibrium,
an equation can be derived relating the derivative with respect to
temperature of the \emph{saturation vapor pressure} $e^*$ (the vapor
pressure of the gas in equilibrium with the liquid water) to the change
in specific volume $\vsp$ between the two phases and the latent heat,
\textit{viz.}
\begin{equation}
\mderiva{e^*}{T} = \frac{L_w}{T\Delta \vsp}; \label{eq:clausius-clapeyron}
\end{equation}
it is the \emph{Clausius-Clapeyron} equation \cite[Chap. 10,
  eq. (10.11)]{adkins--equilibrium}.  Assuming the validity of the perfect-gas
law, and that the specific volume of the liquid phase is negligible in
comparison with that of the vapor phase, the Clausius-Clapeyron equation takes
the form \cite[Chap. 10, eq. (10.12)]{adkins--equilibrium}
\begin{equation}
\mderiva{e^*}{T} = \frac{L_w e^*}{R_vT^2}. \label{eq:clasp}
\end{equation}
Note that
Eqs. (\ref{eq:Lw-theo})--~(\ref{eq:Lw-num}) can be used to integrate 
(\ref{eq:clasp}), yielding
\begin{equation}
  e^*(T) = \left( \frac{T}{T_0}\right)^{\frac{b_L}{R_v}}
  e^*(T_0)
  \exp\left[
    \frac{a_L}{R_v}\left(\frac{1}{T_0} - \frac{1}{T}\right)
    \right], \label{eq:esnelson}
\end{equation}
where $T_0$ is a reference temperature. With $b_L = 0$,
(\ref{eq:esnelson}) bears a strong resemblance to (but is not the same
as) Teten's empirical formula
\citep{murray1966computation,dilley1968computer,stull--meteorology,alduchov1996improved}:
\begin{equation}
e^*(T) = e_0 
       \exp\left[ 
	      \frac{b(T - T_1)}{T - T_2}
	   \right],\label{eq:tetens}
\end{equation}
with $e_0 = 610.78\,\mathrm{Pa}$,  $b=17.2693882\,\mathrm{K^{-1}}$,
$T_1 = 273.16\,\mathrm{K}$ and $T_2 = 35.86\,\mathrm{K}$.  For ice,
the constants change to $b=21.8745584$ and $T_2 = 7.66$.

For most meteorological applications, Teten's formula is more than enough. Note
however that the constants change for saturation vapor pressure over \emph{ice};
also, note that the presence of salts in the water alters $e^*$
significantly. Finally, Richards' equation \citep{brutsaert--evaporation.into} is
\begin{align}
  e^*(T) &= 101\,325\exp\left[
    13.3185t_r - 1.9760t_r^2 - 0.6445t_r^3 - 0.1299t_r^4
    \right]\label{eq:esat-rich}\\
    \mderiva{e^*}{T} &=\frac{373.15}{T^2}e^*(T)\left[
    13.3185 - 3.9520t_r - 1.9335t_r^2 - 0.5996t_r^3  
    \right],\label{eq:dsat-rich}\\
    t_r &= 1 - \frac{373.15}{T}.\label{eq:def-tr}
\end{align}

The concept of saturation now allows for the definition of several humidity
indices based on it. The \emph{relative humidity} $y$ is the ratio of the actual
mixing ratio and the mixing ratio in water vapor saturated air at the same
temperature and pressure:
\begin{equation}
y = \frac{r}{r^*}.
\end{equation}
Note that, because it is specified at the same temperature and
pressure, the density of dry air in the saturated atmosphere
($\rho_{d*}$) is actually smaller than the density of dry air in the
non-saturated atmosphere ($\rho_d$). The gas law equations are
\begin{align}
  p - e &= \rho_d R_d T, &  e &= \rho_v R_v T, \\
  p - e^* &= \rho_{d*} R_d T,  & e^* &= \rho_v^* R_v T.
\end{align}
Therefore,
\begin{align}
  y &= \frac{r}{r^*} =
  \frac{\frac{\rho_v}{\rho_d}}{\frac{\rho_v^*}{\rho_{d*}}} = \frac{\rho_{d*}}{\rho_d} \times \frac{\rho_v}{\rho_v^*}\nonumber \\
  &= \frac{\frac{p-e^*}{R_d T}}{\frac{p-e}{R_d T}}\times
     \frac{\frac{e}{R_v T}}{\frac{e^*}{R_vT}}\nonumber \\
  &= \frac{p-e^*}{p-e}\times \frac{e}{e^*}\nonumber \\
  &= \frac{p(1 - e^*/p)}{p(1 - e/p)}\times \frac{e}{e^*}\nonumber \\
  &\approx (1 - e^*/p)(1 + e/p)\frac{e}{e^*}\nonumber \\
  &\approx \frac{e}{e^*}.\label{eq:relhum}
\end{align}
The last form, (\ref{eq:relhum}), is the one commonly used in
calculations.

\section{Potential temperature}\label{sec:potheta}









Consider the adiabatic expansion of a parcel of air, from a level (higher up)
where the ambient pressure is $p$ down to a level where the ambient pressure is
$p_0$. The first law of thermodynamics is ($\heat$ is heat per unit mass added to the system;
$\work$ is work per unit mass performed on the system)
\begin{equation*}
\md\ine = \dbar\heat + \dbar\work;
\end{equation*}
For an adiabatic ($\dbar\heat = 0$) and reversible process,
\begin{equation}
\md\ine = - p\md{\vsp} \label{eq:adrevproc}
\end{equation}
Differentiation of (\ref{eq:pvRT}) and use of (\ref{eq:def-cv}) (keeping in mind
that $\ine = \ine(T)$ for a perfect gas) produce
\begin{align}
p\md{\vsp} + \vsp\md{p} &= R\md{T}\nonumber\\
c_{\vsp}\md{T} &= \vsp\md{p} - R\md{T}\nonumber\\
(c_{\vsp} + R)\md{T} &= \vsp\md{p} = \frac{RT}{p}\md{p} \nonumber\\
c_p \md{T} &= \vsp\md{p} = \frac{RT}{p}\md{p}\label{eq:adiatm}\\
\frac{\md{T}}{T} &= \frac{R}{c_p}\frac{\md{p}}{p}.
\end{align}
\iflanguage{brazil}{E agora José?}{
We now integrate between the temperature-pressure pairs $(T,p)$ and
$(\theta,p_0)$:}
\begin{equation}
\theta = T \left( \frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}}.\label{eq:def-theta}
\end{equation}
The potential temperature $\theta$, defined above, is therefore the temperature
of a parcel of air brought adiabatically and reversibly from state $p,T$ to a
reference pressure $p_0$.


Equation (\ref{eq:def-theta}) needs to be used with the correct value
of $R/c_p$ in the case of moist air.  This is because in this case the
effective gas constant and the effective specific heat at constant
pressure for moist air depend on the specific humidity $q$.  To see
this, suppose that the total enthalpy (not the \emph{specific
  enthalpy}) for a parcel of air with total mass $m$ can be calculated
by adding the enthalpies of dry and moist air:
\begin{equation*}
m \ent = m_d\ent_d + m_v\ent_v.
\end{equation*}
This produces
\begin{align}
\ent &= \frac{\rho_d}{\rho}\ent_d + \frac{\rho_v}{\rho}\ent_v, \nonumber\\
\ent &= (1-q)\ent_d + q\ent_v,\nonumber\\
c_p &= \left(\parder{\ent}{T}\right)_p 
     = (1-q)c_{pd} + qc_{pv}
     = (1 + 0.84q)c_{pd}\label{eq:cp-moist}
\end{align}
(using tabulated values for $c_{pd}$ and $c_{pv}$).

On the basis of (\ref{eq:Tvdef}),
the effective gas constant for moist air can be written as
\begin{equation}
R = R_d(1 + 0.61q)\label{eq:R-moist}
\end{equation}
(note that this is an alternative interpretation to (\ref{eq:Tvdef}): we are now
changing the gas constant $R$, and keeping the absolute temperature $T$, so that 
\ref{eq:pvRT} still applies.)
Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\frac{R}{c_p} = \frac{R_d}{c_{pd}}\frac{1 + 0.61q}{1 + 0.84q}.
\end{equation}
Using the identity
\begin{equation}
\frac{1 + ax}{1 + bx} \equiv 
1 - (b-a)x + \frac{(b-a)bx^2}{1 + bx} \approx 1 - (b-a)x, \label{eq:good-id}
\end{equation}
we find (for small $x=q$)
\begin{equation}
\frac{R}{c_p} \approx (1 - 0.23q)\frac{R_d}{c_{pd}}\label{eq:Rcpmoist}
\end{equation}
for moist air. Therefore, 
\begin{equation}
\theta = T\left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{(1 - 0.23q)\frac{R_d}{c_{pd}}}
\end{equation}
It is also straightforward to obtain the specific heat at constant volume for
moist air:
\begin{equation}
c_{\vsp} = \left(\parder{\ine}{T}\right)_{\vsp} = (1-q)c_{\vsp{d}} +
qc_{\vsp{v}} \label{eq:cvmoist}
\end{equation}



Finally, these definitions extend to virtual temperature, \emph{but
  not uniquely}, as discussed by \citet[section
  3.2b]{brutsaert--evaporation.into}. The \emph{virtual potential
  temperature} is the virtual temperature that a parcel of moist air
would have if changed adiabatically from its actual state to the
standard pressure $p_0$:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{vp} = T_v \left( \frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{(1 - 0.23q)\frac{R_d}{c_{pd}}}.\label{eq:theta-vp}
\end{equation}
This is slightly different from \emph{potential virtual temperature}, which is
the potential temperature of dry air at the same initial pressure and density,
and which is given by
\begin{equation}
\theta_{pv} = T_v \left( \frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{R_d}{c_{pd}}}.\label{eq:theta-pv}
\end{equation}
The two quantities defined by (\ref{eq:theta-vp}) and (\ref{eq:theta-pv}) are,
evidently, numerically very similar. From here on, we will always use the
virtual potential temperature (\ref{eq:theta-vp}). Also, for simplicity, we will refer
to it simply as $\theta_v$.


\chapter{The dynamical equations for the ABL}
\label{cap:eqcla}




The transport equations express the principles of conservation of total mass,
momentum, energy and  mass of a mixed substance (for instance water vapor) at a point
in space and instant in time. In principle, any fluid mechanics problem can be
solved if we know the constitutive equation (the rate of strain -- stress
relation) and the boundary conditions.



In practice, the problem is much more
  complicated. The momentum equations are non-linear, making the solution
  (either analytical or numerical) much more difficult. Morever, they display
  chaotic behavior, which causes very small differences in the boundary
  conditions to amplify very fast \cite[]{ruelle:where}. Finally, their full
  solution requires the resolution of extremely small scales (the Kolmogorov
  microscales), rendering the problem impossible to solve on the basis of the
  fundamental equations alone, and requiring additional hypothesis regarding
  their closure at some level: this is the well known turbulence closure problem
  \citep{wyngaard--boundary-layer.modeling}.  




\section{Differential operators}\label{sec:diff-op}

It is convenient to adopt a double notation for the position and
velocity vectors, and the basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$, \textit{viz.}
\begin{align}
      \vet{x} &=(x,y,z) \equiv  (x_1, x_2, x_3), \\
      \vet{u} &=(u,v,w) \equiv  (u_1, u_2, u_3), \\
      (\vet{i},\vet{j},\vet{k}) &\equiv 
      (\vet{e}_1,\vet{e}_2,\vet{e}_3).
\end{align}



The equations of fluid mechanics involve four differential operators:
the divergence, the curl, the laplacian, and the operator for the
material derivative. They are:
\begin{align}
   \vet{\nabla} \cdot \vet{u} 
   &= \parder{u}{x} +
       \parder{v}{y} +
       \parder{w}{z}  \nonumber \\

\section{The hydrostatic reference state}

Consider again the first law for an adiabatic atmosphere, as in \ref{eq:adiatm},
repeated here,
\begin{equation}
c_p\md{T_r} = \vsp_r\md{p_r}, \label{eq:adiatm-bis}
\end{equation}
together with the hydrostatic equation
\begin{equation}
\mderiva{p_r}{z} = -\rho_r g.\label{eq:hydrostatic-ref}
\end{equation}
Above, the subscript $r$ means the reference state for a hydrostatic
equation. Together with the state equation (\ref{eq:pvRT}), equations
(\ref{eq:adiatm-bis})--(\ref{eq:hydrostatic-ref}) form a system from which we
can eliminate either $T_r$, $p_r$ or $\vsp_r = 1/\rho_r$. Three equations result
for the reference pressure, temperature or density as a function of $z$ (bear
in mind that in a moist atmosphere $c_p$, $c_v$ and $R$ are given by
\ref{eq:cp-moist}, (\ref{eq:cvmoist}) and \ref{eq:R-moist} respectively):
\begin{align}
T_r(z) &= T_0 - \frac{g}{c_p}z, \label{eq:Trz} \\
p_r(z) &= p_0\left( \frac{T_0 -
  \frac{g}{c_p}z}{T_0}\right)^{c_p/R}\label{eq:prz},\\
\rho_r(z) &= \rho_0\left( \frac{T_0 -
  \frac{g}{c_p}z}{T_0}\right)^{c_{\vsp}/R}.\label{eq:rhorz} 
\end{align}

Figure \ref{fig:pvtref} shows the pressure and density reference states in the
atmosphere as functions of $z$, for $T_0 = 15\,\mathrm{^\circ{C}}$ and $p_0 =
101\,325\,\mathrm{Pa}$. The \emph{scale height} is defined as
\begin{equation}
D = \left| \frac{1}{\rho_0}\mderiva{\rho_r(0)}{z}\right|^{-1}; \label{eq:scale-height}
\end{equation}
it is obtained by extrapolating the straight line with the same slope as the
reference density profile at the surface up to $\rho_r = 0$, as seen in figure
\ref{fig:pvtref}-b.

\section{Mass conservation and the Boussinesq approximation}
}
\label{sec:mass-bouss}


The derivation of the transport equations for $u$, $v$, $w$, $\theta$, $q$,
$\theta_v$, $\ldots$ is a long process involving a fair amount of physics and
mathematics. It can be found in various texts, such as 
\cite{batchelor--introduction}, \cite{bird.etal:transport} and
\cite{landau.lifshitz:fluidmechanics}.  An excellent 
itroduction to the equations in a form often used in the ABL is 
\cite{stull--introduction}'s book.



We will briefly analyze the dynamical equations in light of the
\emph{Boussinesq approximation}. First we define a thickness $\ell$
such that the reference density changes little between $z=0$ and $z =
\ell$. From figure \ref{fig:pvtref}-b, it can be seen that
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta \rho_r}{\rho_0} \le \frac{\ell}{D} \equiv \epsilon_b \ll 1, \label{eq:del-ell-D}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_0 = \rho_r(0)$.
In (\ref{eq:del-ell-D}), we use any appropriately small value of $\epsilon_b$
(say $\epsilon_b = 0.01$), to
define $\ell$ rigorously.

Next we define the \emph{Boussinesq decompositions}
\begin{align}
  u_i  & = 0 + u_{\delta i}, \label{eq:bou-ui}      \\
  \rho & = \rho_r + \rho_\delta, \label{eq:bou-rho} \\
  p    & = p_r + p_\delta, \label{eq:bou-p}         \\
  T    & = T_r + T_\delta. \label{eq:bou-T}
\end{align}
Above, $u_\delta$, $\rho_\delta$, $p_\delta$ and $T_\delta$ are the fluctuations
of velocity, density, pressure, and temperature of an actual (``dynamic'')
atmosphere with respect to the reference hydrostatic state.

\newcommand{\reyd}[1]{\overline{#1} + #1'}

Of course, (\ref{eq:bou-ui})--(\ref{eq:bou-T}) can be further expanded by
means of a Reynolds' decomposition, yielding
\begin{align}
  \reyd{u_i}  & =  \reyd{u_{\delta i}}, \label{eq:bourey-ui}      \\
  \reyd{\rho} & = \rho_r + \reyd{\rho_\delta}, \label{eq:bourey-rho} \\
  \reyd{p}    & = p_r + \reyd{p_\delta}, \label{eq:bourey-p}         \\
  \reyd{T}    & = T_r + \reyd{T_\delta}. \label{eq:bourey-T}
\end{align}


Then, the following relations hold (trivially) between the Reynolds
decomposition and the Boussinesq decomposition:
\begin{align}
\overline{u_i}  & =  \overline{u_{\delta i}}        & u'_i  & =u_{\delta i}',  \label{eq:boumeio-u}\\
\overline{\rho} & = \rho_r + \overline{\rho_\delta} & \rho' & = \rho_\delta',  \label{eq:boumeio-rho}\\
\overline{p}    & = p_r + \overline{p_\delta}       & p'    & = p_\delta',     \label{eq:boumeio-p}\\
\overline{T}    & = T_r +  \overline{T_\delta}      & T'    & = T_\delta',     \label{eq:boumeio-T}
\end{align}
whence
\begin{align}
u_i  & = 0 + \overline{u_{\delta i}} + u',         \label{eq:boufin-u}\\
\rho & = \rho_r + \overline{\rho_\delta} + \rho',  \label{eq:boufin-rho}\\
p    & = p_r + \overline{p_\delta} + p',           \label{eq:boufin-p}\\
T    & = T_r + \overline{T_\delta} + T'.           \label{eq:boufin-T}
\end{align}
Clearly, the notations $\overline{u_{\delta i}}$ and $u_{\delta i}'$,
$\rho_\delta'$, $p_\delta'$ and $T_\delta'$ in
(\ref{eq:boumeio-u})--(\ref{eq:boumeio-T}) are excessive, and will not
be used any further.

Only now we introduce the Boussinesq approximation proper: it is the
assumption that the dynamic density fluctuations $\rho_\delta$ are of
the same order of magnitude of the variation of the reference density
$\rho_r$ within the layer of thickness $\ell$. In view of the
definition of $D$ in (\ref{eq:scale-height}) and (\ref{eq:del-ell-D}),
it is
\begin{equation}
\rho_\delta \sim \rho_0 \frac{\ell}{D} \ll \rho_r, \qquad 0 \le z \le
\ell.
\label{eq:boussapp}
\end{equation}


Now consider the full form for the equation for (total) mass
conservation, which reads
\begin{equation}
\parder{\rho}{t} + \sum_{k=1}^3 \parder{(\rho u_k)}{x_k} = 0.\label{eq:total-mass}
\end{equation}
Its ensemble average is
\begin{equation}
\parder{\overline{\rho}}{t} + \sum_{k=1}^3 \parder{\overline{\rho u_k}}{x_k} = 0.\label{eq:avg-total-mass}
\end{equation}

Expanding all terms with the help of (\ref{eq:boufin-u})--(\ref{eq:boufin-T})
and Reynolds' postulates
(\ref{eq:post-media-media})--(\ref{eq:post-comuta-pop}),
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\parder{\overline{\rho_{\delta}}}{t}}_{\text{I}} +
\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\overline{u_k}\parder{\rho_r}{x_k}}_{\text{II}} + 
\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\overline{u_k}\parder{\overline{\rho_{\delta}}}{x_k}}_{\text{III}} +
\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\rho_r\parder{\overline{u_k}}{x_k}}_{\text{IV}} + 
\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\overline{\rho_{\delta}}\parder{\overline{u_k}}{x_k}}_{\text{V}} + 
\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\parder{\overline{\rho' u_k'}}{x_k}}_{\text{VI}} = 0.\label{eq:massa-media-allorders}
\end{equation}

The order of magnitude of each individual term in the sums above can be
established relatively easily. For that, we need to define in some useful way a
density scale $\tilde{\rho}$ and a velocity scale $\tilde{u}$. Due to
(\ref{eq:boufin-rho}) and (\ref{eq:boussapp}), we will define
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\rho} \sim \overline{\rho_\delta} \sim \rho',\label{eq:tilde-rho}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\tilde{u} \sim \overline{u_k}; \qquad \tilde{\rho}\tilde{u} \sim \overline{\rho'u'_k}.\label{eq:tilde-u}
\end{equation}
With that, one finds
\begin{align*}
\text{I} &\sim \frac{\tilde{\rho}\tilde{u}}{\ell};\\
\text{II} &\sim \frac{\Rho_0\tilde{u}}{D} \sim
\frac{\tilde{\rho}\tilde{u}}{\ell};\\
\text{III} &\sim \frac{\tilde{\rho}\tilde{u}}{\ell};\\
\text{IV}  &\sim \frac{\rho_0 \tilde{u}}{\ell};\\
\text{V}  &\sim \frac{\tilde{\rho}\tilde{u}}{\ell};\\
\text{VI}  &\sim \frac{\tilde{\rho} \tilde{u}}{\ell}.
\end{align*}

The upshot is that all individual terms in I--III, and V--VI, are much smaller
than the individual terms in IV. The \emph{sum} of all three terms in IV must be
of the same order of the other terms, whence
\begin{equation}
\sum_{k=1}^3\parder{\overline{u_k}}{x_k} \sim \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}
\frac{\tilde{u}}{\ell} \ll \frac{\tilde{u}}{\ell} \Rightarrow
\sum_{k=1}^3\parder{\overline{u_k}}{x_k} \approx 0.\label{eq:cont-incompr-media}
\end{equation}
Equation (\ref{eq:cont-incompr-media}) is often first found in fluid mechanics
in the context of mass conservation in an incompressible flow. Here, it is only
\emph{approximately valid} (we are in the context of the Boussinesq
\emph{approximation}); it is valid (at this point) for the mean velocity field
$\overline{\vet{u}}$ only; and \emph{it does not mean that there are no density
  fluctuations}. 

The same approach can be taken for the full momentum equation, which is (for a
Newtonian fluid)
\begin{multline}
  \left[ \parder{(\rho u_i)}{t} + \sum_{k=1}^3 \parder{(\rho u_i u_k)}{x_k}
  +\sum_{k=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3 2 \epsilon_{ijk} \Omega_j u_k \right] =\rho g_i -
  \parder{p}{x_i} +\\
\parder{}{x_i}\left(\lambda \sum_{k=1}^3 \parder{u_k}{x_k}\right)+
  \nu\rho \sum_{k=1}^3 \parder{s_{ik}}{x_k}, \qquad i =
  1,\ldots,3,\label{eq:Navier-Stokes}
\end{multline}
where $\vet{\Omega}$ is the angular velocity of the Earth;
$\sum_{k=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3 2\epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_ju_k$ is the Corilis
acceleration
along $x_i$; $\vet{g} = (0,0,-g)$ is the accelration of gravity, 
and $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity.  Expanding and estimating orders of
magnitude for (\ref{eq:Navier-Stokes}) however, is now a lengthy and somewhat
tedious procedure. Perhaps the most revealing part is the effect that
the Boussinesq decomposition has on the gravity and pressure terms:
\begin{align}
  \rho g_i - \parder{p}{x_i} &= (\rho_r + \rho_\delta)g_i - \parder{(p_r
    + p_\delta)}{x_i} \nonumber \\
  &= \rho_\delta g_i - \parder{p_\delta}{x_i}, \label{eq:gp-remain}
\end{align}
in view of the hydrostatic equation (\ref{eq:hydrostatic-ref}).
Equation (\ref{eq:gp-remain}) shows very clearly that an important
part of the acceleration in the fluid is caused by the differences in
bouyancy caused \emph{Boussinesq fluctuations} in density,
$\rho_\delta$.

If one now proceeds with the Boussinesq and Reynolds decomposition,
followed by averaging and simplification with (\ref{eq:total-mass})
(which we omit, but encourage the reader to undertake!), the final
result is
\begin{equation}
\rho_r\left[\parder{\overline{u_i}}{t} + 
\overline{u_k}\parder{\overline{u_i}}{x_k} +
\parder{\overline{u_iu_k}}{x_k}
\right] -
\left[\overline{\rho_\delta}g_i -
\parder{\overline{p_\delta}}{x_i} \right] \sim\frac{\tilde{\rho}\tilde{u}^2}{\ell}.\label{eq:bouss-quantmov-approx1}
\end{equation}
Each of the individual terms above can be shown to be $\sim \rho_0
\tilde{u}^2/\ell$. The Coriolis terms, which do not appear above, are of the
order of $\rho_0\tilde{u}^2/(\Ro \ell)$ (where $\Ro =
\tilde{u}/(f\ell)$ is the Rossby number, and $f = 2\Omega\sin(\phi)$
is the Coriolis parameter). At the scale of the flow, $\ell$, the
Rossby number is very large and consequently Coriolis effects are
negligible. For the left-hand side of
(\ref{eq:bouss-quantmov-approx1}), therefore, one has
\begin{align}
g\tilde{\rho} &\sim \rho_0 \frac{\tilde{u}^2}{\ell},\label{eq:ordem-g}\\
\frac{\tilde{p}}{\ell} &\sim
\rho_0\frac{\tilde{u}^2}{\ell}\;\Rightarrow\label{eq:ordem-p}\\
\tilde{p} &\sim \tilde{\rho}g\ell.\label{eq:p-rhogl}
\end{align}

Pressure fluctuations are extremely difficult to measure in the
atmosphere. \cite{wyngaard--turbulence.in.the.atmosphere} gives the
estimate $p'\sim \rho_0\tilde{u}^2$; this is the same as
(\ref{eq:ordem-p}). \cite{hauf.finke.ea--ground.pressure.fluctuations}
observed values of $p'$ of the order of $2.5\,\mathrm{Pa}$, which
again is the same order as given by Wyngaard. The main consequence of
(\ref{eq:p-rhogl}) is that the effects of the pressure fluctuations
$p_\delta$ and $p'$ can often be neglected in the atmosphere. 


The order of magnitude of the Boussinesq pressure fluctuations now leads to the
following approximation in the state equation:
\begin{align*}
  p &= \rho R_d T_v, \\
  [p_r + p_\delta] &= [\rho_r + \rho_\delta]R_d[T_{vr} + T_{v\delta}],\\
  p_r + p_\delta &\approx \rho_r R_d T_{vr} + \rho_r R_d T_{v\delta} +
  \rho_\delta R_d T_{vr},
\end{align*}
where the product $\rho_\delta R_d T_{v\delta}$ has been neglected. By
definition, the equation of state, $p_r = \rho_r R_d T_{vr}$ holds for the
hydrostatic reference state; therefore,
\begin{align*}
  p_\delta \approx \rho_r R_d T_{v\delta} + \rho_\delta R_d T_{vr}, \\
  \underbrace{\frac{p_\delta}{p_r}}_{\sim\frac{\tilde{p}}{p_0}}
  \approx \underbrace{\frac{T_{v\delta}}{T_{vr}}}_{\sim \frac{\tilde{T}}{T_0}}
  + \underbrace{\frac{\rho_\delta}{\rho_r}}_{\sim \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}}
\end{align*}
But
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0} &\sim \epsilon_b = 0.01 \qquad\text{by definition},
    \\
    \frac{\tilde{p}}{p_0} &\sim \frac{2.5}{101\,325} = 2.47\times10^{-5} \ll 0.01.
\end{align*}
Therefore, the Boussinesq pressure fluctuations can be safely dismissed. This
leads to a much simpler linear equation of state that does not include pressure,
namely
\begin{equation}
\frac{\rho_\delta}{\rho_r} = -\frac{T_{v\delta}}{T_r}.\label{eq:linstate-rho-T}
\end{equation}

the extension of the Boussinesq approximation to the higher-order,
second-moment equations that are essential to understand the mechanics
of turbulent flow, and which were introduced by O. Reynolds in 1895
\citep{reynolds--b-dynamical}. We will however \emph{assume} that 
the equations for the turbulent fluctuations of velocity and other
quantities are analogous to (\ref{eq:cont-incompr-media}) and
(\ref{eq:bouss-quantmov-approx1}) for the means.

Under these further assumptions, application of Reynolds'
decomposition (\ref{eq:reynolds-decomp}) to the mass and momentum

   & - 
   \underbrace{
      \frac{1}{\rho_r}
      \left(
         \parder{\overline{u'_ip'}{x_j} + \parder{\overline{u'_jp'}{x_i}
      \right)
   }_{\rm VII} -
   \underbrace{
      \overline{
         \frac{p'}{\rho_r}   
         \left(
            \parder{u'_i}{x_j} +\parder{u'_j}{x_i}
         \right)
      }
   }_{\rm VIII} \nonumber \\
   &+
   2\nu\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{%
     \parder{}{x_k}\left[
        \overline{u'_j s'_{ik}} + \overline{u'_js'_{jk}}
     \right]
   }_{\rm IX}   \nonumber \\
   &-2\nu\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{%
       \left[
         \overline{s'_{ik}\parder{u'_j}{x_k}} +
         \overline{s'_{jk}\parder{u'_i}{x_k}}
     \right]
   }_{\rm X} \label{eq:din-covar}%
\end{align}
\end{comment}
In (\ref{eq:din-covar}),
\begin{equation}
s'_{ik} = \frac{1}{2}\left( \parder{u'_i}{x_k} + \parder{u'_k}{x_i}\right) \label{eq:fluct-def-rate}
\end{equation}
is the fluctuation rate of deformation.

The set 
(\ref{eq:din-covar})
is essential to understand the physics of turbulence.
We borrow from \citep{stull--introduction} their meanings: 
\begin{enumerate}
\renewcommand{\theenumi}{\Roman{enumi}}%
\item Is the local rate of change of the covariance $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$.

\item Is the advection of $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$ by the mean wind 
$\overline{u_k}$.


\item Is the \emph{gradient production/destruction term}. A loose interpretation is
that the average profiles are subject to perturbations that generate large-scale
structures that progressively break into smaller structures, and so on. The
larger structures are anisotropic, an evidence of this fact being the very
existence of the mean gradients $\partial \overline{u_j}/\partial x_k$, but the
smaller structures (``eddies'') become more and more isotropic, and their
statistics more homogeneous and stationary.

\item This is the \emph{transport term}: it reflects turbulence's \emph{closure
  problem}: note that in equation (\ref{eq:bouss-quantmov-approx1}) for the mean
$\overline{u_i}$ there are 2\nd-order moments $\overline{u'_iu'_k}$; here, in
the equation for the second-order moments $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$ the
\emph{3\rd-order moments} $\overline{u'_iu'_ju'_k}$ appear, and so on \textit{ad
  infinitum}. Indeed, Reynold's decomposition always leads to more equations than
unknowns to any order. In the atmospheric boundary layer, the divergence of
3\rd-order moments is often important and reflects \emph{non-local}
redistribution of covariances: it adds considerable difficulty in modeling and
understanding its physics.


\item This is the buoyancy term: the buoyancy flux $\overline{\rho'u_j'}$ has a
counterpart in the virtual sensible heat flux $\overline{u'_j\theta_{v}'}$, via
the simplified equation of state (\ref{eq:linstate-rho-T}) that emerges from the
Boussinesq approximation.  Depending on the sign of
$\overline{u'_j\theta_{v}'}$, it can either produce or destroy the covariance
$\overline{u'_iu'_j}$. Production of turbulence by buoyancy makes the atmosphere
more \emph{unstable}, and destrution makes it more \emph{stable}.

\item This is the Coriolis term: it is negligible in the atmospheric boundary
layer \citep{stull--introduction}.

\item Like III, this is a divergence term: it is called the \emph{pressure
  redistribution} term, and also like III it redistributes $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$
without creating nor destroying covariances in the overall flow domain
\cite{tennekes.lumley--first}.




\item This is the \emph{return to isotropy} term
\citep{rotta1951statistische-1,rotta1951statistische-2, hinze--turbulence,
  lumley.newman--returntoisotropy,choi.lumley--returntoisotropy}. Its sum for
the 3 components $\overline{u'_1u'_1}$, $\overline{u'_2u'_2}$ and
$\overline{u'_3u'_3}$ is zero, showing that it acts to reduce the anisotropy of
turbulence, and to nudge the Reynolds stress tensor $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$ to
become isotropic.

\item This term accounts for the molecular diffusion of $\overline{u'_iu'_j}$:
it is very small and can be neglected.

\item This is the viscous dissipation term. It can be shown to be small for the
case $i\ne j$ \cite{wyngaard--boundary-layer.modeling}, but is very important
and always when $i=j$; then, it accounts for the dissipation of turbulence
kinetic energy, and is of the same order of the other important terms in
(\ref{eq:din-covar}), such as gradient production and buoyancy.
\end{enumerate}


An equation that has a central role in the understanding of the physics (or, at
least, the phenomenology) of turbulence is the turbulence kinetic energy (TKE)
equation. It is obtained by setting $i=j$ in (\ref{eq:din-covar}), summing
from 1 to 3, and dividing by two. The quantity
\begin{equation}
\overline{e} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^3 \overline{u'_iu'_i}
\end{equation}
is the kinetic energy of the flow that is due to the turbulent velocity
fluctuations.  The TKE equation is
\begin{multline}
\underbrace{\parder{\overline{e}}{t}}_{\text{I}}
+ \sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\overline{u_k} \parder{\overline{e}}{x_k}}_{\text{II}} =
-\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\overline{u_i'u_k'}\parder{\overline{u_i}}{x_k}}_{\text{III}}
-\sum_{k=1}^3\underbrace{\parder{\overline{e'u_k'}}{x_k}}_{\text{IV}}
\\
+\underbrace{\frac{g}{\theta_r}\overline{w'\theta'_v}}_{\text{V}}
-\underbrace{2\sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{k=1}^3\sum_{l=1}^3\epsilon_{ilk}\Omega_l\overline{u_i' u_k'}}_{\text{VI}}
-\underbrace{\frac{1}{\rho_r} \sum_{i=1}^3\left[ \parder{\overline{u_i' p'}}{x_i}  \right]}_{\text{VII}} \\+
\underbrace{2\nu\sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{k=1}^3\parder{\overline{u_i' s_{ik}'} }{x_k}}_{\text{VIII}}-
\underbrace{2\nu\sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{k=1}^3\overline{s_{ik}'s_{ik}'}}_{\text{IX}}.
\label{eq:ect2}
\end{multline}

In (\ref{eq:ect2}),
\begin{enumerate}[I]
\item is the time rate of change of $\overline{e}$;
\item is the advection of $\overline{e}$ (the advective derivative of $\overline{e}$);
\item is the gradient production term;
\item is the transport term;
\item is the buoyancy production of $\overline{e}$;
\item is the (negligible) Coriolis term;
\item is the pressure redistribution term;
\item is the (negligible) diffusive transport of $\overline{e}$;
\item is the dissipation of TKE.
\end{enumerate}
Note that, on account of the mass conservation equation for the fluctuations,
(\ref{eq:cont-flut}), the return to isotropy term in (\ref{eq:din-covar}) now
sums to zero:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i=1}^3 \overline{\frac{p'}{\rho_r}\parder{u_i'}{x_i}} =
  \overline{\frac{p'}{\rho_r}\left[ \sum_{i=1}^3 \parder{u_i'}{x_i}\right]} = 0.\label{eq:returned}
\end{equation}

Further simplification can be achieved if we assume the conditions under which
the Monin-Obukhov Similarity Theory is valid: horizontal homogeneity,
stationarity, mean flow along the $x$ direction only. Then one has

\begin{align}
0 &= 
-\overline{u'w'}\parder{\overline{u}}{z}
-\parder{\overline{e'w'}}{z}
+\frac{g}{\theta_r}\overline{w'\theta'_v}
-\frac{1}{\rho_r} \parder{\overline{w' p'}}{z}
-2\nu\sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{k=1}^3\overline{s_{ik}'s_{ik}'}\nonumber\\
0 &= 
-\overline{u'w'}\parder{\overline{u}}{z}
-\parder{\overline{e'w'}}{z}
+\frac{g}{\theta_r}\overline{w'\theta'_v}
-\frac{1}{\rho_r} \parder{\overline{w' p'}}{z}
- \epsilon_e
\label{eq:ect-most}
\end{align}
